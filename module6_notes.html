Welfare analysis framework
Goal: build a metric — a "yardstick" — to measure efficiency
Welfare economics asks: how good is this outcome for society? The yardstick will be held up against competitive markets, monopoly, oligopoly, and any other structure we study.
"You don't need to take a course in economics to understand monopolies are not very efficient for most people. Monopoly is great if you're the monopolist — but that's why we have antitrust laws."
Assumption
No government intervention — just two parties: consumers and producers.
Definition
Welfare to economists = efficiency of outcomes, not income transfers or food stamps.
Benchmark
Perfectly competitive market = the best-case outcome. Everything else gets measured against it.
Consumer surplus (CS)
CS = Consumer value − consumer expenditure
CS = area below demand curve, above price P
The demand curve is marginal willingness to pay. Anyone whose willingness to pay exceeds the market price gets a surplus — they would have paid more but didn't have to.
"I'm buying a Snickers bar. In my head it's worth $2.50. I hand over $1. That extra $1.50 — the excess of my value over what I paid — that's consumer surplus."
Price increase effect
When price rises from P₀ to P₁, CS falls. The lost area has two parts: (1) a transfer to producers — consumers now pay more for the same units; (2) a deadweight loss triangle — consumers who would have bought at P₀ no longer buy at all.
Consumer surplus — graph
CS is the triangular area below the demand curve and above the price line P꜀. Every buyer who would have paid more than P꜀ receives a surplus equal to (their WTP − P꜀).
Producer surplus (PS)
PS = Net revenue above what is needed to supply each unit
PS = area below price P, above the supply curve (= ΣMCᵢ)
The supply curve is built from marginal costs — it tells us the minimum price at which each successive unit will be offered to the market. For any unit whose marginal cost is below the market price, the producer earns a surplus on that unit equal to (P − MC).
"I would have sold you that unit for $2. But the market price is $5. That $3 gap? That's a piece of producer surplus. Add all those gaps up across every unit produced — that's total PS."
Key mechanism
Firms are not forced to sell. They choose to supply at the market price because price ≥ MC for all units produced. The vertical gap between the market price line and the supply curve at every output level is the per-unit surplus. Summing across all Q꜀ units gives the triangular PS area.
Producer surplus — graph
PS is the triangular area below the price line P꜀ and above the supply curve. The supply curve (= sum of all firms' MC curves) shows the minimum firms would accept for each unit — anything above that is their gain.
Social gain & the benevolent dictator
SG = CS + PS  — the "size of the pie"
SG = CS + PS  → maximized at Q꜀ where D intersects S (competitive equilibrium)
Social gain aggregates the net benefits to every party in the market. When there is no government, those parties are just consumers and producers. SG is the economist's measure of how efficient an allocation is — not who got what, but how big the total gain was.
Who is the benevolent dictator?
A thought-experiment character introduced to help us reason about efficiency. The BD:
Power
Can dictate any allocation — who produces, who consumes, what price is charged.
Goal
Maximizes total SG — the size of the pie. The BD does not care about the distribution between CS and PS.
Neutrality
$1 of CS counts the same as $1 of PS. No favourites. The BD would never sacrifice pie size to shift slices around.
Logic
A pie of 100 beats a pie of 99 — always. If you want a different split, redistribute later. First, maximise the pie.
"The benevolent dictator is not out to push people around. She just wants to see society get the most efficient outcome. Make the pie as large as possible — then if society wants to redistribute, go ahead and cut it with a very sharp knife later."
Key insight: price only decides who gets the surplus, not the total
At a given output α where WTP = $10 and MC = $2:
If P = $8
CS = 10 − 8 = 2
PS = 8 − 2 = 6
SG = 2 + 6 = 8
If P = $5
CS = 10 − 5 = 5
PS = 5 − 2 = 3
SG = 5 + 3 = 8
The price just slides the split. SG is always the full vertical gap (WTP − MC) = $8. The BD doesn't care whether price is $5 or $8.
The BD's decision rule: produce every unit where WTP > MC
At each potential output level, the BD asks: does this unit generate more value to consumers than it costs society in resources? If yes — produce it. If no — stop.
α to γ → WTP > MC at every unit → each unit adds to SG → produce it
at γ (= Q꜀) → WTP = MC exactly → last unit adds zero net SG → stop here
past γ → MC > WTP → unit destroys SG → don't produce
"Every time we add another unit before γ, I get social gain in excess of social cost. Keep going right. Each time gaining, each time gaining. I don't want to stop short of γ — there's still gain left on the table."

Why the BD supports redistribution — but after, not instead
A progressive income tax, food stamps, housing vouchers — these are all sharp-knife policies that redistribute the pie after the market has baked it as large as possible. The BD supports these redistribution tools. What the BD opposes is shrinking the pie in the name of fairness. You can't cut up a smaller pie and make everyone better off.
BD verdict on competitive equilibrium
The perfectly competitive market delivers exactly what the BD wants — output Q꜀ where D = S, maximising SG. No other output level produces a larger pie. This is why competitive markets are the benchmark against which all other market structures (monopoly, oligopoly) are judged.
What is a monopoly?
A monopoly is a sole supplier with no threat of entry
Competitive firms are price takers — they observe the market price. A monopolist is a price maker — it sets its own price. Since the firm IS the market, firm output q = market output Q.
Competitive firm
Price taker · MR = P · Horizontal demand curve
Monopolist
Price maker · MR < P · Faces full downward-sloping D
Attributes of a monopoly market
1
Single seller
One firm supplies the entire market. The firm is the industry — firm output q equals market output Q. There is no distinction between the firm's demand curve and the market demand curve.
2
No close substitutes
The product has no adequate alternatives. Consumers cannot switch to a comparable good if price rises. This gives the monopolist control over price — if substitutes existed, customers would simply leave.
3
Price maker (not price taker)
The monopolist sets price — it does not take the price as given. It faces the full downward-sloping market demand curve and chooses any (Q, P) combination on that curve. It maximises profit by choosing output, then reading price off the demand curve.
4
MR < P (always)
Because the monopolist must lower price to sell one more unit — and must do so for all existing units — marginal revenue is always below price. For a linear demand P = a − bQ, marginal revenue is MR = a − 2bQ (same intercept, twice the slope).
MR = a − 2bQ < a − bQ = P
5
High barriers to entry
No new competitor can enter the market — whether due to patents, ownership of a critical resource, massive economies of scale, or government licence. This is what distinguishes a monopoly from a firm that is temporarily large. Without barriers, profits would attract entry and erode.
6
Positive economic profit — permanently
Because barriers block entry, positive profits (P > ATC) are not competed away. In a competitive market π → 0 as firms enter. In a monopoly, π > 0 holds in both short run and long run — the monopolist keeps printing money every period.
π = Q × (Pₘ − ATC) > 0 every period

Monopolist's pricing power — choosing any point on D
The monopolist is not constrained to a single price — it can choose any point on D. It picks the output Q*ₘ where MR = MC, then charges the highest price consumers will pay for that quantity (point B). This is the profit-maximising combination.
Why barriers to entry are the defining attribute
Remove any barrier and the monopoly unravels. Positive profits attract new entrants → supply increases → price falls → π → 0. The monopoly can only stay a monopoly as long as entry is blocked. This is why the source of barriers matters so much legally:
Legal / Earned
Patent, superior product (Skill, Foresight & Industry), natural monopoly cost structure. Courts allow these.
Resource Control
Owning the critical input (ALCOA / bauxite). Legally ambiguous — depends on how control was acquired.
Predatory
Dynamiting rivals' refineries (Standard Oil), exclusionary contracts, predatory pricing. Illegal under Sherman Act §2.
"The key defining characteristic isn't just that there's one seller — it's that no one can come in and compete, even when profits are sky-high. That's what makes the distortion permanent."
3 causes of monopoly
1
Natural monopoly
Economies of scale so large that one firm can serve the whole market at lower cost than two or more. Classic example: water utilities. Duplicate pipes under every street is wildly inefficient — so the government grants one company a regulated monopoly.
2
Government grants — especially patents
A patent gives exclusive rights for ~17–20 years to incentivize R&D. Xerox (xerography), pharmaceuticals after FDA trials. Without patents, a competitor could reverse-engineer a copy machine in 5 days and flood the market in 2 weeks — eliminating all incentive to invent.
3
Control of a natural resource
ALCOA acquired mineral rights to most of the world's bauxite (required to make aluminum). No bauxite access = no aluminum production. WWII ended the case before it resolved — the government built its own plants and sold them to Kaiser & Reynolds after the war.
Why MR < P for a monopolist
To sell one more unit, the monopolist must lower price for everyone
At P = $100, 50 units sell. To sell the 51st unit, price drops to $99. The monopolist gets $99 from the new buyer — but also loses $1 on each of the original 50. Net MR = $99 − $50 = $49, not $99.
MR = 99 − 50 = 49  →  MR < P always for a one-price monopolist
MR curve: same intercept as D, twice the slope
With linear demand P = a − bQ, total revenue TR = aQ − bQ². Taking the derivative: MR = a − 2bQ. Same vertical intercept a, but slope is −2b (twice as steep as demand).
If D: P = a − bQ  →  MR = a − 2bQ
Example: D: P = 100 − 6Q  →  MR = 100 − 12Q
"You don't need to use calculus. As long as demand is linear, just remember: MR has the same intercept as D and goes down at twice the slope."

Monopoly equilibrium & profit
Profit maximization: set MR = MC → find Q*ₘ → read price off demand
π = Q × (P − ATC)  > 0 in both short and long run
Unlike competition, positive profits don't erode. No threat of entry means the monopolist earns these returns every period. Xerox earned positive profits for 17 years under patent protection.
Step 1
Draw D and MR (same intercept, twice slope). Draw MC.
Step 2
Find Q*ₘ where MR intersects MC.
Step 3
Go up from Q*ₘ to the demand curve → read off Pₘ.
Step 4
π = (Pₘ − ATC) × Q*ₘ = area of the profit rectangle.
Long-run distinction from competition
In competition: π > 0 → entry → π = 0 eventually. In monopoly: no threat of entry, so π > 0 is permanent. The monopolist just keeps printing money.
Deadweight loss — the social cost of monopoly
Monopolist restricts output from Q꜀ to Qₘ → creates deadweight loss
When the industry merges to monopoly (e.g., J.P. Morgan's U.S. Steel, 1905), the new monopolist raises price and cuts output. Some CS is transferred to PS (the monopolist gets richer), but a triangle of surplus disappears entirely — it's not captured by anyone. That's deadweight loss.
SG_monopoly = CS_M + PS_M < CS_C + PS_C = SG_competitive
CS_M (consumer surplus) Monopoly profit (Pₘ−ATC) PS_M (producer surplus) DWL (deadweight loss)
What happens to each piece?
Part of the old CS is transferred to the monopolist as higher PS (Pₘ > P꜀ on existing units). The DWL triangle is destroyed — no one gets it. The pie shrinks from the competitive size by exactly the DWL triangle.
"The monopolist just takes a lot of output off the market to push up price and line its own pockets. But that triangle in the middle? Nobody gets it. Left on the table. That's the social cost."

Antitrust laws
Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) — the original law
§1 Price fixing
Firms cannot enter any contract in restraint of trade — no price-fixing agreements, no agreed advertising budgets, no collusive behavior of any kind.
§2 Monopolization
Having or attempting to acquire monopoly power is illegal. Must meet two requirements to win a case.
2 requirements to prove monopolization
1
Establish the existence of monopoly power
Must define the relevant market correctly. Glass jar case: company had 90% of glass jars but only ~30% of the containers market. Court dismissed — no monopoly established. Market definition is everything.
2
Rule of Reason — it must be a "bad" monopoly
Supreme Court (Standard Oil, 1911) said monopoly via Skill, Foresight, and Industry is legal — if you have the best product, you deserve your market share. Kodak won on this ground (superior film quality). But Standard Oil lost because of evidence of dynamiting competitors' refineries.
Structural relief — the punishment
If found guilty, the remedy is structural relief: break up the company into competing parts. Standard Oil → ~15 separate firms we know today (Standard, Mobil, Esso, etc.). The goal: restore competitive market structure.
