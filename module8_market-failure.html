What Is a Market Failure?

Core Definition
A market failure occurs when markets that look well-suited to efficient outcomes get derailed along the way — not because of monopoly, but because of information problems. The result: the benevolent dictator's social gain is not maximized.

Key insight: Markets can appear competitive and still produce deeply inefficient outcomes when information is incomplete or unevenly distributed between buyers and sellers.

Type 1 — Monopoly
Already studied. Addressed through antitrust law (except natural monopolies like water mains where duplication makes no sense).
Type 2 — Information Problems
Focus of this module. Includes Winner's Curse, Lemons Markets, Adverse Selection, and Externalities.

The Winner's Curse

"The person who wins the auction is the most overly optimistic bidder out of all the bidders — they realize too late that 99 other experts valued it lower."

— Prof. DeBrock
Real-World Example: Outer Continental Shelf Oil Auctions
Post-OPEC (1973+), the US Government auctioned mineral rights to offshore tracts in the Gulf of Mexico via sealed-bid auctions. Oil companies hired geologists to estimate oil reserves, but deterministic bidding — just using the geologist's raw estimate — led to systematic overbidding and losses. Winners went out, built derricks, dropped pipes — and found far less oil than they paid for.
Bid Distribution — Bell Curve Diagram
1
100 bidders, each with their own geologist's estimate → bids form a bell curve by the Central Limit Theorem (n ≥ 35)
2
The highest bidder wins — but this person is by definition the most overly optimistic out of all n bidders
3
After winning, they realize n−1 other experts were less optimistic — the real value is closer to μ, not the winning bid
4
Overbidding = losses. Oil comes out, but not as much as they paid for. People were using "deterministic bidding" — wrong approach.

Solution — Strategic Bidding (Nash Outcome): Create a bid function conditional on being the winner. If you win, it means n−1 others bid less → revise your estimate downward accordingly before submitting. Never bid your raw expected value directly.

The Lemons Problem (George Akerlof)

Definition: Asymmetric Information
Some market participants have more information than others — and this asymmetry can produce bad outcomes. Akerlof's Nobel Prize paper "The Market for Lemons" showed how incomplete information breaks markets entirely.
Level 1 — Solvable: Sick Crabapple Tree
Homeowner vs. tree surgeon. The surgeon has more information about sick trees, but the homeowner can get multiple quotes to verify. Second opinions correct the asymmetry. Problem is mild and fixable.
Level 2 — Annoying Inefficiency: Curbside Depreciation
Buy a new car Friday. Try to sell it back Tuesday with 180 miles on it. The dealer offers only ~83 cents on the dollar — a ~17% instant depreciation. Why? The dealer knows nothing about those 180 miles; you know everything. They price it as if it might be a lemon. This isn't dealers colluding — it's information economics.
Level 3 — Very Bad: Insider Trading
Why is insider trading illegal? Not just unfairness — because widespread asymmetry makes stock markets illiquid like maple syrup. Everyone wonders what the other side knows → fewer transactions → inefficient, thin markets. The market for information collapses trust in the market itself.
Worked Example: 1968 Dodge Dart
Consumer Reports says: 50% chance "Good" ($10,000 value), 50% chance "Bad" ($5,000 value).
Expected value = $7,500. You offer $6,000 (a lowball). Seller accepts immediately.

Implication: A seller with a good car ($10,000) would never accept $6,000. The seller only accepts because she knows it's worth $5,000 — less than your offer. You just paid $6,000 for a $5,000 car.

Market collapse logic: Knowing this, rational buyers offer only $5,000 (the lemon price). Good car sellers refuse. Only lemons trade. The market for quality cars ceases to exist.

Markets for lemons: transactions either don't happen (good cars won't sell at lemon prices) or happen at inefficiently low prices. The market severely shrinks or disappears entirely.

Adverse Selection — Unraveling Insurance Markets

"Adverse selection means the people selecting your product are not the people that are going to make things work for you."

— Prof. DeBrock
Setup: Health Insurance with One Pooled Rate
An insurer quotes one average rate to 100 people. The actuary calculates it will exactly cover expected claims — works perfectly if everyone stays in the pool. But individuals know their own health; the insurer doesn't. This asymmetry destroys the market.
Adverse Selection — The Death Spiral

ACA Solution — Individual Mandate: The Affordable Care Act requires everyone to buy insurance. This keeps healthy people in the pool, stabilizing the risk distribution. Supreme Court upheld it as constitutional in June 2012. Without it, young healthy adults opt out and the spiral begins.

Externalities — Definition & Types

Definition
A cost (or benefit) imposed on others without economic implications on the agent making the decision. The person causing the effect feels none of the consequences. Also called spillover effects.

Root cause: Lack of well-defined property rights. No one "owns" the air above their house or the water in a river → firms use these as free disposal sites.

Anytime the benevolent dictator is unhappy, the market has not achieved the efficiency we want. The BD just wants to maximize the size of the pie — externalities shrink the pie.

Negative Externality
Your behavior hurts others at no cost to you.

Examples:
• Loud neighbor at 2:30am before your exam
• O'Hare new runway → planes over housing subdivision, property values fall, shingles ripped
• US Steel Gary, IN — SO₂ → more repainting, car rust, lower health outcomes
• Hudson River factories using river as free sewer
• Cuyahoga River in Cleveland caught fire (1960s–70s)
Positive Externality
Your behavior benefits others at no gain to you.

Examples:
• Honeybee farms near orchards — pollinators help both parties
• Home landscaping → neighbors' property values rise (2+ houses down, statistically significant)
• College education → fewer crimes, better health, higher incomes for whole state
• Public universities subsidize tuition to maximize educated population benefit
Historical Fix: The EPA (1970)
The EPA was created because no one owned rivers or air. The Cuyahoga River burning in Cleveland was the tipping point. EPA said: "We now own that river. We own the air over Gary. You can't use them for free anymore." Factories that once drained waste directly into the Hudson River now face real costs for disposal.
📈

Graphing Positive Externalities

MSB = MPB + External Social Benefit

The market demand curve represents Marginal Private Benefit (MPB) — what buyers personally value. With a positive externality, others also benefit. The Marginal Social Benefit (MSB) includes both the buyer's value and the spillover to others. MSB lies to the right of MPB.

Positive Externality — Underproduction & DWL Triangle
Why Underproduction?
The market stops at Q₀ (where S meets MPB). But MSB is higher — more production would benefit society. The benevolent dictator wants output at Q_soc. The gap creates deadweight loss.
Policy Fix: Subsidize
Push output toward Q_soc by making it cheaper to produce or consume:
• Community landscaping grants
• Subsidized public university tuition
• R&D tax credits
📉

Graphing Negative Externalities

MSC = MPC (S₀) + Pollution Cost

Firms only see their Marginal Private Cost (MPC) — wages, materials, factory costs. The Marginal Social Cost (MSC) includes the pollution cost borne by others. MSC lies above and to the left of MPC. Because firms don't pay the pollution cost, they overproduce.

Negative Externality — Overproduction & DWL Triangle
1
Direct control on emissions — regulators place meters on smokestacks, set a legal maximum. Some states enforce this.
2
Tax production of Q — taxing output raises the effective cost curve, shifting S₀ up toward MSC, reducing output toward Q_soc.
3
Tax emissions directly — for every unit of pollution emitted, a fee is charged. The preferred economic option: it internalizes the externality — firms now "see" the cost they were imposing on others.

"Internalize the externality" = make the firm pay for the pollution cost. Their private cost curve shifts up to match MSC. Once they feel the full cost, they naturally reduce output to Q_soc.

Cap & Trade System

"We're putting a price on pollution so people recognize that while it's important to get stuff out of the factory, they're also putting a burden on the community — it's just another input cost."

— Prof. DeBrock
1
Determine the "cap" — Scientists across disciplines determine the maximum acceptable level of a pollutant. You can't set it at zero (that would halt steel production), but you can set a feasible target and reduce it incrementally year over year.
2
Issue permits — Government issues exactly enough permits to match the cap. Each permit authorizes a firm to emit a specific quantity. No permits = no legal pollution. EPA actively monitors smokestacks.
3
Allow permit trading — A firm that wins a new contract and needs to run a third shift can buy spare permits from a firm that doesn't need them. Creates a market that allocates pollution rights efficiently to their highest-value uses.
EPA Annual Permit Reduction Path (SO₂ Cap & Trade, 1993+)
US EPA SO₂ Cap & Trade History
The EPA began cap and trade in 1993 for sulfur dioxide (SO₂) to address acid rain. The Chicago Board of Trade ran annual permit auctions for 13+ years each April. Anyone could buy and retire permits — including the Sierra Club. Today the EPA runs it in-house with eBay-style auction software.

• Firms invested in more efficient furnaces to need fewer permits
• Technology changed — pollution became a real input cost
• Permits became an actively traded commodity (WSJ tracked the price)
• Total SO₂ emissions dropped substantially over the period

Permits can't be zero — the law of conservation of matter means some emissions from any industrial process are unavoidable. The goal is a feasible, declining cap that approaches but never reaches zero.
