What This Module Covers

A bird's-eye view of Module 1 — from scarcity to supply & demand equilibrium.

Core Premise

The Fundamental Problem: Scarcity

Economics is the science that studies the allocation of scarce goods and services. Scarcity isn't just about rare paintings — economists believe everything is scarce, including clean air and fresh water. Scarcity means "there's not enough to go around." This single fact forces every society to adopt some allocation mechanism — a rule for deciding who gets what.

"Economists believe that there's really nothing that we have on this planet that everybody can have all they want of it any time they want."

— Prof. Larry DeBrock
⚖️

Allocation Mechanisms

How do we decide who gets scarce goods? Options include command economies (a king/government decides), markets (prices decide), or queuing (standing in line). Western economies rely primarily on markets + prices.

💰

Prices as Rationing Devices

Prices aren't just numbers — they are rationing tools. High price → fewer buyers → less demand. Low price → more buyers → more competition for supply. They automatically balance supply and demand.

🔄

Opportunity Cost

Every choice means forgoing the next best alternative. Even "free" things have an opportunity cost. A firm that earns $3M must ask: could it earn more doing something else?

📊

Market Equilibrium

The price where quantity demanded = quantity supplied. At equilibrium, there's no tendency for change. External shocks shift curves and force markets toward a new equilibrium.

Scarcity & Allocation

Lesson 1-1.1 — The foundational problem of all economics.

Definition

Scarcity

Scarcity = "Not enough to go around." Something is scarce when not everyone who wants it can have as much as they want at zero cost. Economists believe everything is scarce — even clean air (catalytic converters exist for a reason) and fresh water (aquifers are depleting).

Not Enough → Someone Decides → Allocation Mechanism → Markets / Prices (Western Economies)
Real-World Example · 1990 RAM Prices

In September 1990, a 4 MB RAM chip cost $2,299–$2,599. Today, a chip 128× more powerful (512 MB) sells for $4.59. No government bureau ordered cheaper chips — entrepreneurs flocked to Silicon Valley, competition grew, prices fell. Markets at work.

Allocation Mechanisms

How Societies Decide "Who Gets What"

  • Command Economy — The king or government decides who gets apartments, cars, meat rations. (Historical / Soviet-era example)
  • Markets + Prices — "Western economies." Prices drift up when goods are scarce → some buyers exit → more producers enter → equilibrium restores itself.
  • Queuing — When prices are set to $0, people stand in line instead. Allocation by time cost rather than money cost.
Real-World Example · Duke Basketball (Krzyzewskiville)

Duke's Cameron Indoor Stadium holds <10,000 seats — the smallest arena in Division I basketball. Student tickets are priced at $0. Result? Students camp in tents for 4+ weeks in a designated area called "Krzyzewskiville." The allocation mechanism shifted from prices to queuing — but scarcity didn't disappear, it just manifested as wasted time. "You can't wish away scarcity."

"Price will use its magic to try and get some people out, get more people in, to solve the scarcity issue."

— Prof. DeBrock

Opportunity Cost

Lesson 1-1.2 — Perhaps the single most important concept in this course.

Definition

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost = the value of the next best foregone alternative. Every action you take means you are not doing something else. That "something else" is your opportunity cost. It applies to time, money, resources — everything.

"There's no such thing as a free lunch."

— Milton Friedman (Nobel Prize Winner)
💵

The $5 Bill

A $5 bill has no intrinsic use (you can't eat it), but it embodies the value of everything you could exchange it for. Spending it on coffee means you gave up lunch, or an Uber ride. Every purchase forecloses other purchases.

🚗

The Car Story (Cornell, 1975)

A law student couldn't afford a car. His brother bought one with savings. DeBrock's point: the brother also paid interest — implicit opportunity cost (lost interest on savings). Explicit vs implicit costs are both real.

Application to Firms

Why Good Managers Ask the Right Question

An accountant says: "Boss, we made $3 million profit this quarter!"

A good economist-minded manager immediately asks: "But what could I have made if I deployed these assets differently? Could I earn $4 million making Twinkies instead?"

This is why opportunity cost drives decisions about which industries firms enter or exit. Profitability is always relative to alternatives.

  • Accounting profit ignores opportunity cost.
  • Economic profit = accounting profit minus opportunity cost.
  • Firms exit industries where economic profit is negative (better alternatives exist).
Insight

Watching this video has an opportunity cost too — you could be binge-watching Friends or sitting at a coffee shop. Every moment spent doing one thing is a moment not spent doing the next best thing.

Demand Curves

Lessons 1-1.3 & 1-2.1–1-2.2 — The buyer side of every market.

Definition

The Demand Curve

A demand curve is a locus of points showing how much consumers wish to purchase at different prices. Written as Q = f(P). It is downward sloping — as price rises, quantity demanded falls. As price falls, quantity demanded rises.

Note: Technically, this is the inverse demand curve (price on Y-axis, quantity on X), but economists have used this convention since Alfred Marshall's foundational treatise.

When price rises, each purchase requires giving up more "green sheets of paper" — forgoing more alternatives (opportunity cost). At higher prices, some consumers find the opportunity cost too high and exit the market. At lower prices, new buyers enter and existing buyers purchase more. This is the Law of Demand.
Real demand curves are likely nonlinear (curved). But linear curves give the same intuition and are far more tractable mathematically. Finding the intersection of two straight lines is simple algebra. Nonlinear functions require quadratic solutions. Since the intuition is identical, linearity wins for pedagogy — even PhD programs use linear curves.
Firms estimate demand from data. Grocery loyalty card programs track every purchase — when prices change, the data reveals how quantity responds. Combined with census data (average household composition by zip code), property values (Zillow data), and purchase history, firms build enormous datasets. Statisticians then use econometric techniques to estimate the demand function for each product.
Example

Prof. DeBrock's grocery store loyalty card tracks that when mayonnaise goes on sale, he buys more — but when Johnnie Walker Red Label Scotch rises in price, he doesn't cut back. Different price elasticities for different products, all captured in the data.

Critical Jargon — Don't Mix These Up!

Change in Demand vs. Change in Quantity Demanded

⬅️➡️

Change in Demand

The entire demand curve shifts. Caused by: changes in tastes, income, prices of related goods, consumer expectations. E.g., a government study showing tomatoes prevent heart disease → whole curve shifts right (increase in demand).

↕️

Change in Quantity Demanded

Movement along a static demand curve due to a price change only. E.g., gasoline tax raises price → consumers buy less gas. The curve didn't shift — consumers just moved to a different point on the same curve.

Exam Trap

If you're asked "what happens if gasoline taxes rise?" — the correct answer is a decrease in quantity demanded (movement along the curve), NOT a "decrease in demand" (shift). The gas itself hasn't changed; only its price did.

Supply Curves

Lesson 1-2.3 — The seller side of every market.

Definition

The Supply Curve

Supply = how much firms are willing to sell at different possible prices. Written as Q = h(P). It is upward sloping — higher prices induce firms to supply more. Why? Because producing more output is progressively more expensive (hiring more workers, overtime pay, etc.), so firms need higher prices to justify greater output.

Critical Jargon — Mirrors Demand!

Change in Supply vs. Change in Quantity Supplied

⬅️➡️

Change in Supply

The whole supply curve shifts. Caused by: input cost changes, technology improvements, weather events, new firms entering/exiting. E.g., a drought shifts the corn supply curve to the left — less corn at every possible price.

↕️

Change in Quantity Supplied

Movement along a static supply curve due to a price change only. E.g., price drops from P₀ to P₁ → firms supply less (Q₁ < Q₀). Same supply conditions; different point on the curve.

Real-World Example · Corn Drought

A big drought hits. Corn farmers can no longer supply the same quantities they could before. At the original price P₀, they can only put Q₁ on the market (less than before). The entire supply curve shifts left/back toward the price axis. This is a change in supply, not a change in quantity supplied. To restore equilibrium, price must rise.

Producing more output costs progressively more. Firms must hire extra workers (often at overtime rates), use less efficient equipment, acquire additional inputs. These escalating costs mean firms need higher prices to justify putting more on the market. Eventually, supply curves connect to full cost curves — production functions, marginal costs — which are developed in later modules.

Market Equilibrium

Lesson 1-2.4 — Where supply meets demand, and what happens when things change.

Definition

Equilibrium

Equilibrium = "No tendency for change." In a market, equilibrium is the price P* and quantity Q* where quantity demanded = quantity supplied. Both buyers and sellers are satisfied; neither group has any incentive to push prices higher or lower.

📦

Surplus (Price Too High)

Firms produce more than consumers want to buy. Unsold goods pile up. Rational response: firms lower prices to attract buyers and reduce production. Price drifts down toward equilibrium.

🏃

Shortage (Price Too Low)

Consumers want more than firms are willing to supply. Buyers bid up the price. At higher prices, more firms supply more output and some buyers exit. Price drifts up toward equilibrium.

Proof Method

Proof by Contradiction

To prove that Pₑ is the only equilibrium price, assume any other price and show it creates either a surplus or shortage — meaning there IS a tendency to change. Therefore no other price can be an equilibrium.

  • Any price above Pₑ → surplus → price falls back to Pₑ
  • Any price below Pₑ → shortage → price rises back to Pₑ
  • Only at Pₑ is there no tendency for change → it is THE equilibrium
Real-World Example · Florida Orange Juice Freeze

Ice covers Florida orange groves. Florida represents ~⅓ of US OJ supply. Supply curve shifts sharply left. At the old price P₀, consumers still want Q₀ OJ — but now only a fraction is available → shortage. Consumers bid up the price. Some exit the market ("back to Diet Coke"). Remaining suppliers divert whole oranges into juice production. A new, higher-price equilibrium forms. A trained analyst sees the frost on TV and immediately knows: "Supply curve shifted left — OJ price is going up."

"You can't just wish away scarcity. You have to put some mechanism in place — who makes the call."

— Prof. DeBrock
The Big Picture

Reporter vs. Analyst

A reporter tells you what happened: "The frost ruined the oranges." An analyst understands the mechanism: the supply curve shifted left, creating a shortage, which caused prices to rise, inducing new supply and reduced demand, settling at a new equilibrium with higher price and lower quantity.

This course's goal is to turn you into the analyst — someone who can predict the direction of price and quantity changes before the market settles.
