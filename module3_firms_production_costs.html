Unit 3-1

The Theory of the Firm

3-1.1 An Economist's Production Function ›

Firms exist to maximize profits — the residual of revenues after costs are subtracted. But where do costs come from? There are no "magic elves" building products overnight. Every firm must purchase inputs, combine them efficiently, and convert them into outputs that consumers will pay for.

Economists model this with a production function. Using the real-world example of the massive Kraft plant in Champaign, IL — which produces half of all macaroni and cheese sold in the US and is the sole source of Velveeta in North America — we can observe inputs (workers, eggs, glass jars, labels) entering and outputs (jars of mayonnaise) leaving.

Production Function
q = f(L, K)
where q = output, L = labor, K = capital

The two inputs economists use: Labor (L) — all variable inputs like workers, raw materials — and Capital (K) — fixed assets like the factory building and assembly lines.

3-1.2 Types of Firms ›

There are three general classes of business organization. Each carries different risk, tax, and ownership implications.

Sole Proprietorship
Single owner. Owner reports profit on Schedule C. Simple to form, but owner bears full personal liability for business debts.
Partnership
Group of owners sharing profits per agreed split. Can be very small (Larry & Brian's tree service) or massive (global accounting firms with hundreds of partners).
Corporation
Independent legal entity. Owners are shareholders. Limited liability — owners can only lose the value of their equity stake, not personal assets.

Data from the 2019 Statistical Abstract of the United States (2013 data) shows the striking contrast:

Type Count Share of Firms Revenue Revenue Share Liability
Sole Proprietorships 24.1M 71.9% $1.3T 3.9% Full
Partnerships 3.5M 10.4% $5.1T 15.3% Full
Corporations 5.9M 17.6% $26.9T 80.8% Limited
Trade-off: Why not always incorporate?

Corporations face double taxation: corporate income tax on profits, then shareholders pay income tax again when dividends are distributed. Sole proprietors only pay once. If risk is low (e.g., selling quilts on Etsy), incorporation often isn't worth it.

3-1.3 & 3-1.4 The Behavioral Rule: Maximize Profits ›

Whether for-profit or not-for-profit, every firm faces the same fundamental efficiency constraints. The goal: maximize the residual of revenue over cost.

Profit (π) — Economic Profit
π = TR − TC
TR = Total Revenue  |  TC = Total Cost (includes opportunity cost)
Total Revenue = P × Q
Economic vs. Accounting Profit

Accounting profit = TR − explicit costs only. Economic profit = TR − explicit costs − opportunity costs. A hot dog vendor making $78,000/year has a negative economic profit if they gave up a $100,000 salary to do so. Economists care about opportunity cost because it reveals where resources should flow.

3-1.5 Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns ›
Key Definition · Marginal

Economists use the word marginal to mean change in. Marginal cost = change in cost. Marginal product = change in output. For calculus users: marginal = the derivative.

In the short run, as we vary labor while capital is fixed, output follows a predictable pattern. The short-run production function is S-shaped:

  • Increasing returns region: Adding workers raises output proportionally faster. (Adding a 2nd assembly-line worker eliminates side-switching inefficiency.)
  • Inflection point: The transition between regions.
  • Diminishing returns region: Each additional worker still adds output, but less than the previous worker did.
Short-Run Production Function
The Law (no known exceptions)

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Product: after some point, extra inputs will raise output less than previous inputs did. This is a short-run phenomenon arising from fixed capital. It applies universally — from tree trimming to Boeing 737 assembly — and there are no known counterexamples. A Nobel Prize awaits whoever finds one.

Short run vs. Long run is about time, not duration. For Larry's Tree Trimming, the long run is 30 minutes (drive to Home Depot for a second chainsaw). For a gasoline refinery, it's 4 years from groundbreaking to first output. What matters is whether you can change all inputs.

Short Run vs. Long Run
SR: q = f(L; K̄)    ← K fixed
LR: q = f(L, K)     ← all inputs variable
Unit 3-2

Cost Theory

3-2.1 · 3-2.2 Total Cost: Fixed & Variable Components ›

Production costs come from inputs. The production function tells us how many inputs are needed for any output level. Pricing those inputs gives us cost. The total cost curve is built by adding two components:

Total Cost
TC = FC + VC
FC = Fixed Cost (brick & mortar, capital) — does not change with q
VC = Variable Cost (labor, materials) — rises with q

Fixed Cost (FC) is a horizontal line — whether Kraft makes 0 or 100,000 jars of mayonnaise today, the cost of the factory and assembly lines is the same.

Variable Cost (VC) is the inverse of the production function, scaled by the wage rate. Because of the law of diminishing marginal product, the VC curve is S-shaped: rising slowly at first (increasing returns region), then steeply (diminishing returns region).

The Total Cost curve is the VC curve shifted up vertically by the amount of FC. Every point on TC is exactly FC above VC.

Total Cost = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost
3-2.3 – 3-2.5 Average Cost Curves (ATC, AFC, AVC) ›
Average Total Cost
ATC = TC / q = AFC + AVC
AFC = FC/q  (average fixed cost)
AVC = VC/q  (average variable cost)

Average Fixed Cost (AFC) is a rectangular hyperbola — always declining. As output grows, the fixed cost is spread over more units. It asymptotically approaches zero but never reaches it. At very low output, AFC explodes toward infinity.

Average Variable Cost (AVC) is U-shaped. The ratio VC/q equals the slope of a ray from the origin to the VC curve. That slope falls until the ray is tangent to VC (the minimum of AVC), then rises. This U-shape is entirely a creature of the law of diminishing marginal product.

Average Total Cost (ATC) = AFC + AVC. Also U-shaped, but its minimum lies to the right of AVC's minimum. At high output, ATC and AVC converge because AFC → 0.

Average Cost Curves
Practical Meaning of ATC

ATC = per-unit cost. If Kraft's ATC for a jar of mayonnaise is $3.27 but the market price is only $3.10, they lose money on every jar. The goal is to sell above ATC.

3-2.6 Marginal Cost: The Most Important Curve ›

Marginal cost is the single most important cost concept for profit maximization — saved for last, but used constantly from here forward.

Marginal Cost
MC = dTC/dq = ΔTC/Δq = slope of TC
How much does one more unit of output cost to produce?

For the Kraft plant: marginal cost of one more jar of mayonnaise = extra eggs + extra milk + glass jar + label + some workers' time. No change in fixed costs — the factory is already standing.

Incremental Cost

Business practitioners often call this incremental cost. The Wall Street Journal uses both terms. They mean the same thing: how much extra does it cost to make one more unit?

Since fixed costs don't change with output, the derivative of FC with respect to q is zero. This means marginal cost has absolutely no relationship to fixed costs — a key insight with powerful implications (as we'll see with regulations and taxes).

MC and Fixed Costs
MC = dVC/dq    (dFC/dq ≡ 0)
Fixed costs are sunk in the short run — MC ignores them entirely
3-2.7 – 3-2.9 The Complete Family of Cost Curves ›

The MC curve is also U-shaped — the slope of TC falls in the increasing-returns region and rises steeply in the diminishing-returns region. Two critical intersection rules:

The Bowling Average Rule

When marginal > average, average is being pulled up. When marginal < average, average is being pulled down. Therefore, MC must pass through the minimum of both ATC and AVC. (If your bowling average is 150 and you roll 180, your average rises. Roll 120, it falls.)

Complete Family of Cost Curves
MC ∩ ATC
MC always intersects ATC at ATC's minimum. This is the most efficient per-unit production point.
MC ∩ AVC
MC also intersects AVC at AVC's minimum — by the same marginal-pulls-average logic.
MC ∩ AFC
Point α — completely indeterminate. MC has no relationship to fixed cost, so this intersection is meaningless.
If FC ↑
ATC shifts up. AVC unchanged. MC unchanged. Minimum of ATC still occurs at MC intersection — just higher.
