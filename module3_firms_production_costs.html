Unit 3-1

The Theory of the Firm

3-1.1 An Economist's Production Function ›

Firms exist to maximize profits — the residual of revenues after costs are subtracted. But costs don't appear by magic. There are no magic elves building products overnight. Every firm must purchase inputs, combine them efficiently, and convert them into outputs consumers will pay for.

Using the Kraft plant in Champaign, IL as a running example — which produces half of all macaroni and cheese sold in the US and is the sole source of Velveeta in North America — we observe inputs (workers, eggs, glass jars, labels) entering and outputs (jars of mayonnaise) leaving. Economists model this with a production function.

Production Function q = f(L, K) q = firm output  |  L = labor (variable)  |  K = capital (fixed in SR)
Capital uses K because C is reserved for cost functions.
3-1.2 Types of Firms ›

Three general classes of business organization exist, each carrying different risk, tax, and ownership implications.

Sole Proprietorship
Single owner. Reports profit on Schedule C. Simple to form — but the owner bears full personal liability. Drop a tree on someone's BMW and you personally owe the $300,000.
Partnership
Group of owners sharing profits by agreed split. Can be tiny (Larry & Brian's tree service) or massive (global accounting firms with hundreds of partners). Still carries full personal liability.
Corporation
Independent legal entity. Owners are shareholders. Limited liability — max loss = equity stake. But faces double taxation: corporate profits taxed first, then dividends taxed again.

The Double Taxation Trade-off

Corporations offer limited liability but face double taxation: corporate income tax on profits, then shareholders pay income tax on dividends. If you're selling quilts on Etsy, the risk is low and incorporation often isn't worth it.

3-1.3 · 3-1.4 The Behavioral Rule: Maximize Profits ›

Whether for-profit or not-for-profit, every firm faces identical efficiency constraints. Non-profits simply redirect their residual toward a cause instead of distributing it to owners. The behavioral rule is the same: maximize profits (or surplus).

Profit Equation π = TR − TC TR = P × Q TC includes opportunity costs — this is economic profit, not accounting profit
Economic vs. Accounting Profit

Accounting profit = TR − explicit costs only. Economic profit = TR − explicit costs − opportunity costs. A hot dog vendor earning $78,000/year has positive accounting profit — but negative economic profit if they gave up a $100,000 salary. Economists care about opportunity cost because it reveals where resources should flow.

3-1.5 Marginal Product & Law of Diminishing Returns ›
Key Term · Marginal

Economists use marginal to mean change in. Marginal cost = change in total cost. Marginal product = change in output per additional input. In calculus terms: marginal = the derivative. In plain terms: it's the slope.

Marginal Product (MP)
The amount by which output increases when an input is increased by a small amount (often one unit), holding other inputs fixed.
Marginal Product of Labor (MPL)
Additional output produced by adding a small amount of labor, holding capital fixed. In calculus terms: MPL is the derivative of the production function with respect to L.
MPL — Calculus Definition MPL = ∂q/∂L = ∂f(L, K̄)/∂L The slope of the production function at any point. As L increases, how does MPL behave? → It eventually falls (Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns).

With capital fixed in the short run, the production function q = f(L; K̄) is S-shaped as we vary labor:

  • Increasing returns region: MPL is rising — each extra worker adds more than the previous one. Specialization gains dominate.
  • Inflection point: MPL is at its peak — the rate of output growth is about to slow.
  • Diminishing returns region: MPL is falling — each additional worker still adds positive output, but less than the one before. The production curve flattens.
Short-Run Production Function
q = f(L; K̄)
Inflection
This is a Law — No Known Exceptions

Law of Diminishing Marginal Product: after some point, extra inputs will raise output less than previous inputs did. No counterexample exists across any industry — consulting, auto manufacturing, baristas, tree trimming. A Nobel Prize awaits whoever finds one. It is a short-run phenomenon caused by overutilizing the variable input relative to fixed capital.

Short Run vs. Long Run SR: q = f(L ; K̄) ← some inputs fixed (K̄ notation signals fixed K) LR: q = f(L, K) ← all inputs variable How long is the long run? It is industry and context specific — and can be very long. Larry's Tree Service: ~30 min. A gasoline refinery: ~4 years from groundbreaking to first output.
Notation Note · K̄ vs f(L;K)

Two equivalent notations signal that capital is fixed. Some texts write K̄ (K with a bar over it) inside the function: f(L, K̄). Prof. DeBrock uses a semicolon: f(L; K) — everything to the right of the semicolon is parametric (held fixed). Treating K as fixed in the short run is a simplification of the standard model; in reality, any input that cannot be changed on short notice qualifies as "fixed."

Unit 3-2

Cost Theory

3-2.1 · 3-2.2 Total Cost: Fixed & Variable Components ›

The production function tells us how many inputs are needed for any output level. Pricing those inputs gives us cost. Total cost has exactly two components in the short run:

Total Cost TC = FC + VC FC = Fixed Cost (factory, assembly lines) — does not change with q
VC = Variable Cost (labor, materials) — rises as output rises

Fixed Cost (FC) is a horizontal line. Whether Kraft makes 0 or 100,000 jars of mayonnaise today, the cost of the factory is unchanged. Fixed costs are sunk in the short run.

Variable Cost (VC) is the inverse of the production function scaled by the wage rate. Its S-shape reflects the production function: costs rise slowly (increasing-returns region), then steeply as diminishing returns kick in.

The Total Cost curve is VC shifted vertically up by exactly FC. Every point on TC is precisely FC dollars above VC.

3-2.3 – 3-2.5 Average Cost Curves: ATC, AFC & AVC ›
Average Total Cost (per-unit cost) ATC = TC / q = AFC + AVC AFC = FC/q  |  AVC = VC/q

Average Fixed Cost (AFC) is a rectangular hyperbola — always declining, asymptotically approaching zero. At very low output it explodes toward infinity; it never touches the horizontal axis.

Average Variable Cost (AVC) is U-shaped. AVC at any q = slope of the ray from the origin to the VC curve at q. That slope falls until the ray is tangent to VC (minimum AVC), then rises. This U-shape is a direct consequence of diminishing marginal product.

Average Total Cost (ATC) = AFC + AVC. Also U-shaped, with its minimum to the right of AVC's minimum. At high output, ATC and AVC converge as AFC → 0.

Practical Meaning of ATC

ATC = per-unit cost. If Kraft's ATC is $3.27 per jar but the market pays $3.10, they lose money on every jar. The firm must price above ATC to earn positive economic profit.

3-2.6 Marginal Cost: The Most Important Curve ›

Marginal cost is the most important cost concept in economics — it drives the profit-maximization rule in every market structure.

Marginal Cost MC = dTC/dq = ΔTC/Δq = slope of TC How much does one more unit cost to produce? Also called "incremental cost" in business.

For Kraft: marginal cost of one more jar of mayonnaise = extra eggs + milk + glass jar + label + fractional workers' time. No change in fixed costs — the factory is already paid for.

The MPL ↔ MC Inverse Relationship

If labor is the only variable input, the cost of producing a little more output is simply the labor required to do it. This creates a direct, inverse link between MPL and MC:

When MPL is High
Each worker produces a lot of extra output → you need fewer workers per extra unit → MC is low.
When MPL is Low
Each worker produces little extra output → you need more workers per extra unit → MC is high.
MPL Rising
Increasing returns region — output per worker is growing, so cost per unit of output is falling. MC slopes downward.
MPL Falling
Diminishing returns region — output per worker is shrinking, so cost per unit is rising. MC slopes upward. This is why MC is U-shaped.
Formal Inverse Relationship MC = w / MPL w = wage rate. Because production and cost are inversely related, MPL and MC are also inversely related. When MPL peaks (inflection point of production function), MC is at its minimum.
Fixed Costs Have Zero Effect on MC

MC = dTC/dq = dFC/dq + dVC/dq = 0 + dVC/dq. The derivative of a constant is zero. A new city tax, rent increase, or any rise in fixed costs has absolutely no effect on marginal cost. One of the most important and counterintuitive results in cost theory.

3-2.7 – 3-2.9 The Complete Family of Cost Curves ›

The MC curve is also U-shaped — its slope equals the slope of TC: falling during increasing returns, rising steeply during diminishing returns. MC is always positive (producing more always costs more).

The Bowling Average Rule

When marginal > average, average is pulled up. When marginal < average, average is pulled down. If your bowling average is 150 and you roll 180, your average rises. Roll 120, it falls. Therefore MC passes through the minimum of both ATC and AVC. This is provable with calculus but intuitive from the bowling analogy.

MC ∩ ATC minimum
MC always passes through the minimum of ATC — the most efficient per-unit production point.
MC ∩ AVC minimum
By identical logic, MC passes through the minimum of AVC, at a lower output level than min ATC.
MC ∩ AFC (point α)
Completely indeterminate. MC has no relationship to fixed costs. This intersection is economically meaningless.
If Fixed Cost ↑
ATC shifts up. AFC shifts up. AVC unchanged. MC unchanged. Minimum ATC still occurs at MC intersection — just higher.
