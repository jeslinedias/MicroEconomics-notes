What this module is about
Big picture

Module 3 taught us cost curves. Module 4 asks: given an externally set price and those costs, what output should the firm choose? And when losses are unavoidable in the short run, should the firm keep producing or lock the door?

Individual firm decisions Short-run optimization Shutdown conditions Economic vs. accounting profit
Two things the firm knows
1
Its own cost curves
MC, ATC, AVC — everything from Module 3. The firm can map how costs change at every possible output level.
2
An exogenously given price P₀
The firm is a price-taker — it has zero control over the price the market sets. It can only optimize given that price.
"A farmer may not like it, the farmer wishes the price of corn was $2 higher — but the farmer has no control over it. That price is set by the Chicago Board of Trade. We're talking about taking a fixed price, which you have no control over, and optimizing given that price and given the costs you are aware of."
— Prof. DeBrock, Lesson 4-1.1
Why even non-profits care

The profit-maximizing model applies equally to non-profit organizations. They want to maximize "residuals" — surpluses that fund their cause (a hospital, an environmental mission). Maximizing residuals requires running the most efficient operation possible. The math is identical.

Short run vs. long run
Short Run

Fixed inputs (plant, equipment) cannot change. The firm is stuck with its fixed cost. It cannot exit an unprofitable industry — only choose whether to operate or shut down temporarily.

Long Run

All inputs are variable. If Π < 0 the firm can eventually exit. If Π > 0 new firms enter. The long run is when the market reaches equilibrium — but that's Module 5 territory.

MR = MC
The universal rule for profit maximization
Building the profit equation
Profit defined
Π = TR − TC
TR = P₀ × q

Total Revenue is a straight line through the origin — slope = P₀. Because price is fixed and constant, every additional unit of output adds exactly P₀ to revenue. The slope never changes.

Why MR equals the price
Marginal Revenue = slope of TR Key identity
MR = dTR/dq = d(P₀·q)/dq = P₀

Marginal revenue is the extra revenue from one more unit. Since P₀ is constant, every new unit brings in P₀ — no more, no less. On the graph, MR is a horizontal line at the price level P₀.

"If I increase output by one unit, how does my revenue change? Well, if I increased output by one extra q, what do I get? P zero. What if I increased output again? P zero. So every time I increase output, my revenue goes up by exactly the same amount."
— Prof. DeBrock, Lesson 4-1.2
The geometric proof of MR = MC
Maximizing the vertical gap

Profit is the vertical distance between TR and TC. That gap is maximized at the output where the slopes of the two curves are equal.

1
Low output — curves diverging
Slope of TR > slope of TC → gap is still growing → keep increasing output.
2
At the peak gap — slopes equal
Slope of TR = slope of TC → MR = MC → this is q*. Maximum vertical gap.
3
Past q* — curves converging
Slope of TC > slope of TR → MC > MR → each extra unit costs more than it earns → gap shrinking → stop.
Calculus confirmation (optional)
First-order condition
dΠ/dq = 0 → dTR/dq − dTC/dq = 0 → MR = MC

Setting the first derivative of profit to zero finds extreme values. The rule MR = MC emerges directly. (A second derivative test confirms it's a maximum, not a minimum — covered on the next page.)

Profit function shape — summary
At q = 0
−F₀
Pure fixed cost loss. Zero revenue, zero variable cost, full FC hit.
At qα and qβ (break-even)
Π = 0
TR = TC exactly. Two zero-profit points bracket the profit hill.
At q*
Π max
Peak of the profit hill. Largest vertical gap between TR and TC.
The α vs. β problem
Two intersections — only one is the maximum Watch out

A horizontal MR line crosses the U-shaped MC curve at two points. Both satisfy MR = MC, but they have opposite effects.

Point α (left — MC falling)
Point β (right — MC rising)
Profit minimum
Profit maximum ★
Past α: MR > MC → profits rising. Don't stop here — keep going.
Past β: MC > MR → each extra unit costs more than it earns. Stop exactly here.
The rule
Always choose MR = MC where MC is upward-sloping
In practice, textbook graphs omit the downward-sloping portion of MC entirely.
Reading profit off the graph
From TC = q × ATC to the profit rectangle
ATC = TC/q → TC = q × ATC
Π = TR − TC = P₀·q* − ATC·q* = q*·[P₀ − ATC]
1
Find q* — where MR = MC on the upward MC slope
2
Drop a vertical line to the ATC curve at q*
The ATC value at q* gives you per-unit average cost.
3
Height = P₀ − ATC at q*
The vertical gap between the price line and the ATC curve.
4
Width = q*
The horizontal distance from zero to optimal output.
5
Rectangle area = Profit
q* × [P₀ − ATC] — positive if P₀ > ATC, negative if P₀ < ATC.
Two profit outcomes
✓ Positive profit
P₀ > ATC at q*

Price line sits above ATC at optimal output. Green rectangle = profit above opportunity cost. "Good life."

✗ Negative profit (loss)
P₀ < ATC at q*

ATC curve sits above price line. Red rectangle = economic loss. Firm earns less than its next-best alternative.

"Profit is just quantity times the per unit markup. Price minus average total cost is really the per unit markup on the product. We multiply that per unit markup times the number of units we sell — that's our profit."
— Prof. DeBrock, Lesson 4-2.2
Worked example — the flat tax
City imposes a $10,000 flat tax on all businesses Case study

A lump-sum tax, regardless of how much the firm produces, is a pure addition to fixed cost. It has no effect on variable cost or marginal cost.

→
Fixed cost: F₀ becomes F₀ + $10,000
The entire cost curve shifts up at every output level.
→
Marginal cost: unchanged
MC = dTC/dq. The derivative of a constant is zero — fixed cost drops out. MC curve stays exactly where it was.
→
ATC shifts upward: ATC = AFC + AVC
AFC = F/q goes up → ATC curve shifts up. AVC is unchanged.
→
q* is unchanged — but profits fall
Since MR and MC didn't change, optimal output stays the same. But now ATC may sit above P₀, turning the profit rectangle negative.
What options does the firm have?
❌ Cannot raise prices
Price is exogenously given — the firm is a price-taker
Farmers complain bitterly, but they cannot post a different price than the Board of Trade.
❌ Cannot find lower costs immediately
If cheaper technology existed, the firm would already be using it
❌ Cannot exit in the short run
Fixed inputs are locked in — stuck with the lease, the plant, the equipment
"You call your broker and say find a buyer. I might not be able to get it for a week, it might be a month." — DeBrock
Only two choices remain
1. Produce at MR = MC (while losing money)   OR   2. Shut down (q = 0, wait to exit FC)
Economic profit vs. accounting profit
Why economic loss is the real signal

Economic profit subtracts opportunity cost — what the firm's owner could earn doing something else with the same resources. Accounting profit only subtracts explicit costs.

Economic Π > 0
Stay & expand
Earning above opportunity cost. No reason to leave. Others may enter.
Economic Π < 0
Exit signal
Earning less than next-best alternative. In LR: must exit. In SR: stuck — must decide between produce or shut down.
Shut down if P₀ < AVC
Keep producing if P₀ ≥ AVC — even while making a loss
Deriving the shutdown condition step by step
1
Core logic: shut down if TR < VC
If total revenue doesn't even cover variable costs, staying open makes losses larger than shutting down. The firm can't recover any fixed cost contribution by operating.
2
Substitute TR = P₀ · q* and VC = AVC · q*
From the definition: AVC = VC/q → VC = AVC × q
3
Divide both sides by q* (q* > 0)
P₀ · q* < AVC · q* cancels to P₀ < AVC
TR < VC → P₀·q* < AVC·q* → P₀ < AVC ✓
The intuition — factory wages example
"Suppose your workers come in and you had to pay them $40,000 in wages for that day. But the output they make you can sell for $45,000. You're only paying $40,000 and they made $45,000 — that's $5,000 extra you can put towards that fixed cost, which you wouldn't have if you decided to produce zero."
— Prof. DeBrock, Lesson 4-2.4
Keep producing (TR ≥ VC)

Workers cost $40K, output worth $45K. Opening the doors generates $5K towards fixed cost. Still losing money overall, but less than if you stayed closed.

Loss = FC − (TR − VC)
Shut down (TR < VC)

Workers cost $40K, output only worth $30K. Opening the doors loses another $10K on top of fixed costs. Lock up.

Losses = FC + (VC − TR)
Fixed costs are irrelevant to the shutdown decision
Sunk costs don't drive the choice

Whether you shut down or stay open, fixed costs must be paid. They are sunk. The shutdown question is purely: does operating cover its own variable costs? If yes, every dollar of TR above VC chips away at the fixed cost burden — you're better off open.

"That fixed cost is sitting in the corner of the room like a 900-pound gorilla. It's a million-dollar fixed cost. You're going to be paying it whether you produce or not. The only question is: do your workers contribute enough to offset some of it?"
— Prof. DeBrock, Lesson 4-2.5

All key formulas
Π = TR − TC
TR = P₀ × q
MR = P₀   (price-taking firm)
Profit max: MR = MC (upward-sloping MC only)
TC = q × ATC   (from ATC = TC/q)
Π = q* × [P₀ − ATC]
Shutdown: P₀ < AVC
ATC = AFC + AVC
Decision flowchart
1
Find q* → MR = MC, on the upward-sloping portion of MC
2
Is P₀ > ATC at q*?
YES → Produce at q*. Positive economic profit. No further analysis needed.
3
Is P₀ between AVC and ATC at q*?
YES → Produce at q*. Accept losses — you still cover variable costs and contribute to fixed cost.
4
Is P₀ < AVC at q*?
YES → Shut down. Produce q = 0. Wait for long run to exit the industry.
Curve positions cheat sheet
ATC
AFC + AVC
U-shaped. Sits above AVC at all outputs. MC passes through its minimum from below.
AVC
VC ÷ q
U-shaped. Sits below ATC. MC passes through its minimum. Converges to ATC at high q.
MC
dTC/dq
U-shaped. Contains no FC information. Crosses both AVC and ATC at their minimums.
Common exam mistakes
⚠ α vs β confusion
MR = MC has two solutions — use only the upward-sloping MC intersection
α is a profit minimum (MC falling). β is the profit maximum (MC rising). The downward portion of MC is usually not even drawn.
⚠ Lump-sum tax doesn't shift q*
A flat fixed-cost addition moves ATC up but leaves MC and q* unchanged
Only changes to variable cost (i.e. per-unit costs) shift MC and therefore q*.
⚠ Loss ≠ shutdown
A firm rationally produces at a loss whenever TR ≥ VC
Fixed costs are sunk — you pay them either way. As long as operating covers variable costs, it's worth staying open in the short run.
⚠ Short run vs. long run
In LR: Π < 0 means exit. In SR: you cannot exit — only shut down temporarily.
"Shutdown" means q = 0 while still paying fixed costs, waiting for the long run. It is not the same as exiting the industry.
