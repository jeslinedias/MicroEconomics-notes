Overview & Goals

Core Idea
Oligopoly markets have a small number of large rivals whose strategic choices directly affect each other — unlike perfect competition (too small to matter) or monopoly (no rivals). Capturing conjectural interdependence requires collusion theory and game theory.
Learning Objectives
✓Describe how and why cartels and collusive organizations are formed
✓Model cartel behavior — three key tasks
✓Understand antitrust law (Sherman Act §1) and its role in correcting distortions
✓Describe the game theory approach in non-cooperative situations
✓Apply Nash equilibrium as a solution concept

The Oligopoly Problem

Conjectural Interdependence
Honda must conjecture Toyota's response when considering a 10% output increase. Toyota could match it (depressing prices further), hold steady, or cut back. The problem loops: what does Honda think Toyota thinks Honda thinks Toyota thinks…
Two Solution Paths
Path 1 Collusion
Firms coordinate output/prices — act like a monopoly. OPEC is the canonical example. Illegal in the US under Sherman Act §1.
Path 2 Game Theory
Formally model strategic interactions. Born in math, adopted widely. Models equilibrium without coordination.
"When Honda thinks about increasing production by 10%, Honda has to think — I wonder what Toyota's going to do." — Prof. DeBrock

Collusion & Cartels

Setup — OPEC 1973
Competitive oil price = $3/barrel (1945–1973). OPEC wanted $10–$12 by restricting output. Three tasks must be solved.
Figure 2 — Left: market reduces Q from Qc to Q* raising price from $3 to P*. Right: at firm's quota q*, the cartel price P* exceeds the firm's marginal cost — creating the "incentive to cheat" gap.
The Three Cartel Tasks
1
Determine Q*_fix and P*_fix
Find the output level that maximizes cartel profit — act like a monopolist (set MR = ΣMC). Hard politically: members disagree on how aggressive to be. Saudi Arabia vs. smaller OPEC members.
2
Build a quota system
Assign each firm i a quota q*_fix,i so that Q*_fix = Σq*_fix,i. Politically brutal — Saudis cut 15M → 7.5M barrels/day to make it work. Smaller states resisted any cuts.
3
Construct a policing mechanism
Detect: OPEC used satellites to measure tanker waterline depth — heavier load = lower waterline = cheating caught.
Deter: Punishment must be credible. "We'll flood the market" was not credible — no one would kill the golden goose.
Figure 3 — Welfare analysis. Cartel restricts output to Q*, raising price to P*. The gold DWL triangle (vertices at Q*/P*, equilibrium, and Q*/supply) represents welfare permanently destroyed. CS shrinks to the small blue triangle; PS becomes the green trapezoid.
Sherman Act §1 — Antitrust
✗
Illegal per se
No "rule of reason" defense. Proving the fix happened = automatic conviction.
!
Criminal penalties
Since late 1970s: executives go to jail, not just company fines. Career-ending deterrence.
Limit: Sovereign Nations
Sherman Act has no jurisdiction over sovereign nations. OPEC countries cannot be prosecuted — their agreements are international treaties.

Game Theory — Interactive Diagram

Game theory formally models strategic interactions between rational players. Each player chooses a strategy to maximize their own payoff — knowing that rivals are doing the same.
Two key classifications shape how we solve a game.
The entry game is an extensive-form (sequential) game. The entrant moves first; the incumbent responds. The incumbent's goal: make entry look unprofitable before it happens.
Two bank robbers caught and separated. Each must independently choose: Confess or stick to the Alibi. The DA sets payoffs so confessing is always individually rational — even though both would be better off staying silent.
Extensive Form Sequential
One firm moves, rival responds. Modeled as a decision tree. Entry game: incumbent vs. potential entrant. Best for analyzing commitment and credible threats.
Normal Form Simultaneous
Both firms announce simultaneously. Solved with payoff matrices. Honda & Toyota output announcements. Best for single-shot strategic interactions.

Why Both Cheat
B cheats if A cheats: $10 > $0. B cheats if A cooperates: $50 > $30. Cheat dominates regardless. Same logic applies to A. Result: (Cheat, Cheat) = $10 each — even though (Cooperate, Cooperate) = $30 each.

Nash Equilibrium

Definition
"Do the best you can, given what your rival is doing." Drop into a cell. Ask each player: "Given your rival's strategy, do you want to stay?" If both say yes → Nash. If either says no → not Nash.

Key Terms Glossary

oligopolyMarket with 2 to some small number of large firms, each able to directly affect rivals. Neither pure competition nor monopoly.
conjectural interdependenceFirms must guess how rivals will respond to their actions — the core oligopoly challenge. Leads to the "infinite regress" problem.
cartelCollusive arrangement coordinating output/prices to act like a monopoly. OPEC is the canonical example. Illegal in the US under Sherman Act §1.
Q*_fix / P*_fixThe cartel's chosen output and resulting price. Q*_fix < Q_competitive forces price above the competitive level.
incentive to cheatAt quota output, each firm's marginal cost is below the cartel price. The profit gap P* − MC creates an irresistible temptation to secretly produce more.
deadweight lossThe welfare triangle destroyed by cartel pricing. Output restricted below Qc means some mutually beneficial trades never occur — welfare simply vanishes.
Sherman Act §1US antitrust law prohibiting restraint of trade. Price fixing is illegal per se — no "rule of reason" defense. Criminal penalties since the late 1970s.
extensive formSequential-play game. One firm moves, the rival responds. Modeled as a decision tree. Captures commitment and credible threats (e.g. entry game).
normal formSimultaneous-play game. Both firms act at once. Solved with a payoff matrix. Honda and Toyota simultaneous output decisions.
prisoner's dilemmaA game where individual rationality leads to collective irrationality. Both players have a dominant strategy that makes both worse off than cooperation would.
dominant strategy"One play is always correct regardless of what your rival chooses." No need to know rival's probability — the dominant play always wins.
Nash equilibrium"Do the best you can, given what your rival is doing." A cell where no player wants to deviate. May or may not coincide with dominant strategy equilibrium.
defensive advertisingFirms spend heavily on ads to prevent losing customers — not to gain new ones. Classic prisoner's dilemma: both advertise heavily, both earn less than if both held back.
