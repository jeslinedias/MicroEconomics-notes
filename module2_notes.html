LESSON 2-1

Direct Government Intervention

Governments intervene in markets for many reasons — safety, environment, fairness. In this module the focus is on interventions that directly alter prices. There are two main forms:

⬇ Direct Price Controls
  • Government declares the legal price
  • Market participants must comply
  • Two types: floors & ceilings
💰 Taxes & Subsidies
  • Government taxes raise production costs
  • Subsidies act as negative taxes
  • Prices change indirectly via supply shift
The government says, "I'm going to announce the price, and that will be... basically I'm the dictator and I'm setting this price and that's what it's going to be because I can do it, I have government authority."
— Prof. DeBrock on Direct Price Controls
2-1.3

Price Floors

📐
Definition — Price Floor
A minimum price set by government. The price may go above the floor but can never go below it. For the floor to be meaningful ("binding"), it must be set above the natural market equilibrium price.

Price Floor Diagram — Binding Floor Creates a Surplus

A binding floor (P_floor > P₀) creates a surplus: consumers buy only α but producers want to sell β. The government must absorb the difference.

🥛
Real-World Example — U.S. Dairy Milk (1960s–2015)
The U.S. government maintained price floors for fluid milk at the dairy barn level until January 2015. At the floor price:

• Consumers bought only Q^D_F (quantity demanded)
• Farmers supplied the larger Q^S_F
• The government purchased the surplus and converted it into "government cheese" — distributed at free giveaway events nationwide

The government's total outlay = P_floor × (Q^S_F − Q^D_F) = area of the surplus rectangle
⚠️
Key Insight — For a Floor to Be "Binding"
The floor price must be above the free-market equilibrium price P₀. If set below P₀, the market naturally stays above the floor — it has no effect ("non-binding").
What Happens in a Price Floor Market
  • 1
    Government announces minimum price above equilibriume.g., "milk price can never go below $5/gallon"
  • 2
    Surplus emergesProducers supply more than consumers demand at the high floor price
  • 3
    Downward pressure is blockedIn a free market, surplus lowers price. The floor prevents this.
  • 4
    Government must buy the surplusWithout a buyer, the surplus has no market. The government steps in as a purchaser.
  • 5
    Long-run distortionsDairy farmers scaled up production under the guarantee. When floors ended in 2015, many struggled to rebalance herd sizes.
2-1.4

Price Ceilings

🏠
Definition — Price Ceiling
A maximum price set by government. The price may go below the ceiling but cannot exceed it. For it to be binding, the ceiling must be set below the natural equilibrium price P₀.

Price Ceiling Diagram — Binding Ceiling Creates a Shortage

A binding ceiling (P_ceil < P₀) creates a shortage: consumers want Q^D_C but firms only supply Q^S_C at the low ceiling price.

🏙️
Real-World Example — Rent Control in NYC
Manhattan one-bedroom apartments market-clear at ~$4,100–$4,300/month. When city councils set rent ceilings at, say, $900/month:

• Hundreds of applicants line up for a single available apartment (literal lines at 4:30 AM observed in California)
• Landlords remove units from market, invite family members in, or let quality deteriorate
• Non-price rationing emerges: landlords choose tenants by income certainty (dual-income professionals) rather than need
• The well-intentioned policy often fails to help its target beneficiaries (low-income families)
The very people you were hoping to protect by offering a rent price you could actually afford, even if you're working minimum-wage and trying to get a bunch of kids through school — they end up not getting in those places very often.
— Prof. DeBrock on Rent Control Unintended Consequences
Floor vs. Ceiling — Quick Comparison
Feature Price Floor Price Ceiling
What it sets Minimum price Maximum price
Binding when... Floor > Equilibrium Price Ceiling < Equilibrium Price
Market outcome Surplus (overproduction) Shortage (underproduction)
Classic example U.S. dairy milk price support, minimum wage Rent control (Manhattan), gas price caps
Government action Must buy surplus to maintain price Must allocate scarce supply (queuing, rationing)
LESSON 2-2

Indirect Intervention — Excise Taxes

⛽
Definition — Excise Tax
A per-unit tax imposed on the sale of a good. Every unit sold triggers a fixed tax payment (e.g., $1.00 per gallon of gasoline). Common on: gasoline, alcohol, cigarettes, tires, plastic bags.

Unlike direct price controls, excise taxes work indirectly — they raise sellers' costs, which shifts the supply curve upward, which changes the equilibrium price.

🚗
Gasoline Tax Example — Champaign, IL
When you buy a gallon of gas in Illinois, you pay four separate excise taxes:
1. Federal gas tax  2. State gas tax  3. City gas tax  4. County gas tax
Plus: an Illinois tax on underground storage tanks (to fund leak cleanup)

Common folk intuition: "If gas is $3 and we add $1 tax, it'll cost $4." But that's often wrong — it depends on the elasticity of demand.
How to Model an Excise Tax
  • 1
    Start at original equilibrium (S₀ ∩ D₀)Free-market price P₀ and quantity Q₀
  • 2
    Tax raises seller's cost by exactly $T per unitSellers now need $T more to supply any given quantity — new cost is layered on top of existing costs
  • 3
    Supply curve shifts up vertically by $TEvery point on S₀ moves up by T, creating new supply curve Sₜ (parallel to S₀)
  • 4
    New equilibrium at Sₜ ∩ D₀New price Pₜ is higher; new quantity Qₜ is lower. The vertical gap between Sₜ and S₀ = T exactly.

Excise Tax — Supply Shift & New Equilibrium

A $1 excise tax shifts S₀ up to Sₜ. The new equilibrium price is $3.75 — consumers pay $0.75 more, producers keep $0.25 less. The $1 tax is split between both parties.

💡
Critical Insight — Price Does NOT Rise by the Full Tax
98% of people surveyed think a $1 tax will raise the price by exactly $1. This is usually wrong.
The actual price increase depends on the slope of the demand curve. Only if demand is perfectly vertical (perfectly inelastic) does the consumer absorb 100% of the tax.
2-2.3

The Incidence of Taxation

⚖️
Definition — Tax Incidence
Who actually bears the economic burden of a tax. Even if a tax is legally levied on sellers, the economic burden can be shared between buyers and sellers depending on elasticity.
Tax Incidence Formula
Consumer Incidence = Pₜ − P₀
Producer Incidence = P₀ − (Pₜ − T)
Consumer incidence + Producer incidence = T (the full tax)
📊
Worked Example — $1 Tax, Original Price $5.00
P₀ = $5.00  |  T = $1.00  |  New equilibrium Pₜ = $5.55

Consumer incidence = $5.55 − $5.00 = $0.55 (55%)
Producer incidence = $5.00 − $4.55 = $0.45 (45%)
(Producers net only $4.55 after sending $1.00 to government)

The $1 tax is split: consumers are worse off by 55¢, producers are worse off by 45¢.
How Elasticity Determines Who Bears the Burden
🡅 Steep (Inelastic) Demand
  • Consumers must have the product regardless of price
  • Quantity barely changes after tax
  • Consumers bear most of the tax burden
  • Example: gasoline — almost entire $1 passes to consumers at ~$3.97
🡆 Flat (Elastic) Demand
  • Consumers have many substitutes, leave market if price rises
  • Quantity drops sharply after tax
  • Producers bear more of the burden
  • Firms must absorb the cost to keep buyers
🔑
Golden Rule of Tax Incidence
The steeper the demand curve, the more of the tax consumers pay. The flatter the demand curve, the more producers absorb. This is why governments love to tax goods like cigarettes and gasoline — inelastic demand means massive revenue with little behavioral change.
LESSON 2-3

Elasticity

Elasticity gives us a precise, numerical way to describe how "responsive" quantity demanded is to a price change. It replaces vague language ("steep curve") with a calculable ratio.

Price Elasticity of Demand
E = %ΔQ / %ΔP = (ΔQ/Q) ÷ (ΔP/P) = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q)
Inverse of slope × price-to-quantity ratio
🎓
Real-World Puzzle — Higher Tuition, More Applications
At the University of Illinois, tuition rose ~70% over a decade — yet applications and enrollment increased. How?

This reflects two forces: (1) a rightward demand curve shift (more people value college degrees), and (2) relatively inelastic demand for higher education at elite universities — students don't easily substitute a prestigious degree.
📐
Why Elasticity Is Always Negative (And We Use |E|)
Because of the Law of Demand: price ↑ → quantity ↓. So the numerator and denominator always have opposite signs → ratio is always negative.
Economists take the absolute value |E| to avoid negative number headaches and compare magnitudes directly.

Key Determinants of Elasticity
🔄 Availability of substitutes (more → more elastic) 💰 Necessity vs. luxury (necessity → more inelastic) 📅 Time horizon (more time → more elastic) 💵 Share of budget (larger share → more elastic) 🏷️ Product definition (narrow → more elastic)
2-3.3

Elasticity Along a Linear Demand Curve

Elasticity Formula Expanded (for Linear Demand)
E = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q)
Term 1: ΔQ/ΔP = inverse of slope → CONSTANT along a linear demand
Term 2: P/Q → CHANGES at every point along the curve
💡
Key Theorem — Elasticity Changes Along a Straight Line
On a linear demand curve:
• The slope (ΔP/ΔQ) is constant throughout
• But P/Q changes at every point
• Therefore elasticity E changes at every point

Result: The upper half of any linear demand curve is elastic; the lower half is inelastic. The midpoint is unit elastic (|E|=1).

Elasticity Zones Along a Single Linear Demand Curve

Every linear demand curve has an elastic upper half (where P/Q is large) and an inelastic lower half (where P/Q is small). The midpoint is unit elastic.

🧩
Why Steep Curves Still Look "Inelastic" — The Trick
When Prof. DeBrock draws a very steep demand curve for Market I, he's actually showing you only the lower portion of a longer linear curve. That lower portion is indeed inelastic.

Similarly, a very flat curve shows you mostly the upper portion — which is elastic.

Mental shortcut to keep: "Steep curve → think inelastic. Flat curve → think elastic." — it's a useful heuristic even though technically every linear demand has both zones.
🔮
Nonlinear Demand & Constant Elasticity
Economists can construct nonlinear demand curves where the curvature exactly offsets the change in P/Q — resulting in constant elasticity at every point. These are called "constant elasticity demand curves." We won't derive them in this course, but they're an important tool in advanced analysis.
