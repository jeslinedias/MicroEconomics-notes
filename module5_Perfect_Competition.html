4 conditions for perfect competition
1
Large number of relatively small buyers & sellers
No single player has market power. Example: take the largest corn farmer in Illinois and triple their output — zero price impact.
2
Homogeneous (identical) products
Generic, undifferentiated goods. Corn is corn — Illinois, Brazil, or Canada. Not craft beer or branded cereal.
3
Free entry & exit
No regulatory barriers. Not costless — you still build a plant. But no rules blocking you. Contrast: Champaign liquor licenses are capped by the mayor.
4
Perfect information
Prices are well-known. Corn price is quoted 15×/day on Illinois radio. Without this: two-tier pricing emerges (Smithsonian kiosk "tourist trap").
Real-world example that fits all 4
Corn market
Millions of producers, identical product, farmers freely switch between corn & soybeans each season, continuous public price data.
Key implication — price takers
Why
All 4 conditions together mean no firm can influence price. The market sets it; firms observe it.
Effect
Price = marginal revenue for each firm. The demand curve facing an individual firm is perfectly flat (horizontal).
Analogy
If the market price for corn is $3.70, charging $4 means zero sales. Charging less is pointless — you can sell everything at $3.70.
Core profit equation
π = TR − TC = P·q − ATC·q = q(P − ATC)
(P − ATC) is the per-unit markup. Multiply by output q to get total profit. A $0.50 markup on each jar × jars sold = profit.
π > 0
P > ATC. Positive economic profit — rectangle sits below the MR line.
π < 0
P < ATC. Firm loses money but may still operate if P ≥ AVC (short run).
Individual firm supply curve
Supply curve = MC curve at or above min AVC
For any quoted price, the firm jumps to its MC curve at that price level. Below min AVC → shut down (q = 0). Market supply is the horizontal sum of every firm's MC curve:
S = ΣNi=1 MCᵢ   (at or above AVC)

Profit π can be > 0, < 0, or 0 π = 0 (economic)
"Only when economic profit equals zero do we have stability. Nobody inside wants out, nobody outside wants in."
Important distinction
π = 0 means economic profit is zero, not accounting profit. A firm can report $20M accounting profit and still have π = 0 if the best alternative use of those resources also earns $20M. Opportunity cost is built in.
Entry & exit dynamics
π > 0 → Outsiders enter → S shifts right → Price falls → π eroded to 0
π < 0 → Insiders exit → S shifts left → Price rises → π returns to 0
"If profits are positive in the short-run this is going to attract entry from people in other sectors… they will just keep coming in until they've basically collapsed the returns in your sector to the returns in their sector."
External shock walkthrough — demand increase
Start
LR equilibrium. D₀ = S₀, π = 0. ATC tangent to MR₀.
Shock
New study: product cuts cancer risk 20%. D shifts right to D₁.
SR new eq.
Price jumps to P₁, output rises to Q₁. Firms earn positive π. (SR only.)
LR adjustment
Entry flows in, S shifts right to S₂. Price falls back to P₀. π → 0. New LR eq. at Q₂ > Q₀.
LR result: same price P₀, more total output Q₂, more firms
"Think of that ball bearing at the bottom of the bowl. Something kicks it and it rolls everywhere. Eventually it comes to rest again — that's when profits are driven back to zero."

ConditionDecisionReasoning
P ≥ AVC Operate at MR = MC Revenue covers variable costs; residual offsets some fixed cost
P < AVC Shut down (q = 0) Can't cover variable costs — losing money on every unit produced
P < ATC but P ≥ AVC Operate (SR), exit (LR) Fixed costs are sunk in SR; call your broker to get out of the lease
